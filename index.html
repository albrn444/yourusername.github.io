Assignment 3: Constraint Satisfaction & State Space Search

Objective

Solve the Missionaries and Cannibals problem using BFS. Demonstrate state space search, constraint tracking, and solution path analysis.

Problem Definition

Three missionaries and three cannibals must cross a river using one boat. Constraints: (1) boat holds max 2 people, (2) cannibals never outnumber missionaries on either bank.

Solution Path (11 Steps)

State Representation: (M_left, C_left, boat_side)
boat_side: 1=left, 0=right

Step 1:  (3, 3, 1) ← Initial
Step 2:  (3, 1, 0) - Send 1C+1M right
Step 3:  (3, 2, 1) - Send 1C left
Step 4:  (3, 0, 0) - Send 2C right
Step 5:  (3, 1, 1) - Send 1C left
Step 6:  (1, 1, 0) - Send 2M right
Step 7:  (2, 2, 1) - Send 1M+1C left
Step 8:  (0, 2, 0) - Send 2M right
Step 9:  (0, 3, 1) - Send 1C left
Step 10: (0, 1, 0) - Send 2C right
Step 11: (1, 1, 1) - Send 1C left
Step 12: (0, 0, 0) ← Goal

Analysis

Search Strategy: Breadth-first search explored the state space systematically, guaranteeing the shortest solution path (11 steps).

Constraint Propagation: State validity checked at each step: cannibals cannot outnumber missionaries on either bank.

Key Learning: State space search is powerful for problems with clear state representations and transitions. BFS guarantees optimality for unweighted search spaces.

Assignment 5: Constraint Satisfaction Problems (CSP) - Zebra Puzzle

Objective

Solve the Zebra Puzzle (Exercise 6.7) using CSP methodology with AC-3 constraint propagation. Demonstrate problem modeling, constraint encoding, and systematic solution through constraint propagation.

Problem Setup

Five houses with different colors, nationalities, drinks, candies, and pets. 14 clues determine a unique solution. Questions: (1) Who owns the zebra? (2) Who drinks water?

CSP Formulation

25 Variables: One per (attribute, value) pair. Each represents: "In which house does this attribute-value combination occur?"

Domain: Each variable ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5}

Constraints:

  • 5 Alldiff constraints (one per category)
  • 2 Unary: Norwegian = 1, Milk = 3
  • 8 Binary Equality: Englishman = Red, Spaniard = Dog, etc.
  • 1 Arithmetic: Green = Ivory + 1
  • 3 Adjacency: |Norwegian - Blue| = 1, |KitKat - Horse| = 1, |Hershey - Fox| = 1

Solution via AC-3 Propagation

================================================================================
ZEBRA PUZZLE SOLUTION
================================================================================

House           1               2               3               4               5
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Color          yellow          blue            red             ivory           green
Nationality    norwegian       ukrainian       englishman      spaniard        japanese
Drink          water           tea             milk            oj              coffee
Candy          kitkat          hershey         smarties        snickers        milkyway
Pet            fox             horse           snails          dog             zebra

================================================================================
ANSWERS:
================================================================================

✓ The JAPANESE (house 5) owns the ZEBRA
✓ The NORWEGIAN (house 1) drinks WATER

================================================================================

Propagation Analysis

Step 1-2: Unary constraints (Norwegian=1, Milk=3) immediately reduce domains.

Step 3-7: Adjacency and equality constraints cascade through the space, eliminating impossible assignments.

Step 8-11: Remaining values determined uniquely through constraint interaction.

Key Insights

Representation Drives Efficiency: Choosing (attribute, value) → house representation made most clues encode as binary constraints, perfect for AC-3. Poor representations would require complex logic or SAT encoding.
Propagation Power: AC-3 solved a space of 5^25 ≈ 10^17 possibilities through intelligent constraint propagation—no backtracking needed. This exemplifies how CSP methodology scales intelligently.

Conclusion: CSPs are powerful for logic puzzles and combinatorial problems. Declarative constraint specification combined with systematic propagation algorithms solves what would be intractable through brute-force search.

10. Portfolio Reflection Addendum

The Midterm Exam and LLMs: A Shift in Perspective

At the beginning of this course, I viewed AI primarily through classical methods: search, logic, and expert systems. The midterm exam's analytical problems on LLMs and hybrid systems forced me to reconsider what intelligence means in an era of generative AI.

I learned that LLMs achieve impressive capabilities through statistical methods rather than explicit reasoning. This realization deepened my appreciation for hybrid systems that combine LLM capabilities with structured, verifiable reasoning processes.

The Essay Question: Is AI Real Intelligence?

Through my forum discussion and team essay, I grappled with this essential question. My analysis revealed that AI systems demonstrate functional intelligence—they adapt, learn, and solve problems effectively. However, whether they possess genuine understanding or consciousness remains philosophically open.

What became clear is that AI intelligence and human intellect differ fundamentally. This distinction shapes how we should design and deploy AI responsibly.

Overall Learning: Integration and Responsibility

Over ICOM5015, my understanding evolved from viewing AI as techniques to recognizing it as a framework for designing systems that interact responsibly with the real world. The combination of high exam scores (98.59% final), comprehensive programming assignments, and critical reflection on ethics has prepared me to implement AI responsibly.

Key Takeaway: AI works best when we leverage its strengths—pattern recognition, optimization, rapid processing—while maintaining human oversight in domains requiring judgment, ethics, or accountability.
